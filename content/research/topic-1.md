## Overview

As a theorist, I use concepts from Statistical Physics, Inormation Theory and Deep Learning to understand various aspects of neural dynamics, cognition and consciousness.

### Self-Organized Criticality in the Brain

Just like every living system self-organizes in response to the environment and exhibits adaptability, so does the brain. According to statistical physics, there exists a space between order and disorder where special proprties such as long range correlations, scale invariance and optimal information processing arise. The brain criticality hypothesis suggests that the brain self-organizes close to the space of this critical dynamics to learn and adapt optimally. 

I have been exploring this questions in both human fMRI studies and mesoscopic calcium imaging data in mice. This work has revealed how the brain's critical dynamics is altered in different states of consciousness and how it can be harnessed to improve the generalization capabilities of artificial neural networks.

### Altered States of Consciousness

ASCs such as sleep, anesthesia and psychedelic states are characterized by profound changes in subjective experience and brain dynamics. I have been studying the information-theoretic signatures that characterize these states and its implications for understanding creativity, mental health and the nature of consciousness itself.

I complement these empirical studies with computational models of brain dynamics to understand the mechanistic basis of these altered states and their potential therapeutic applications.

### Neuro-AI

Inspired by the brain's remarkable ability to learn and adapt, I am also exploring how principles of self-organized criticality and emergence can be harnessed to improve the generalization capabilities of artificial neural networks. This work has implications for both for our understanding of the brain and the development of more robust and adaptable AI systems.
